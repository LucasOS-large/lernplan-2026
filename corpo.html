Module 1

Introduction to Corporate Finance

What is a firm · Investment & Financing decisions · Agency Problem · Hurdle Rate

🏢 What is Corporate Finance?
Why this matters: Every financial decision a firm makes boils down to two questions — where to invest? and how to pay for it? Corporate Finance gives us the tools to answer both rigorously, always with the goal of maximising the value of the firm for its owners.
INVESTMENT DECISION (CAPEX)
Which real assets should the firm acquire? — factories, patents, software, R&D.

Criterion: Accept if the project's return > Hurdle Rate, i.e. NPV > 0.
FINANCING DECISION
How should those assets be paid for? — equity (shares), debt (bonds, loans), or a mix.

Question: What capital structure minimises the cost of capital?
💰 The Cash-Flow Cycle — How Money Moves
Why this matters: The financial manager's job is to keep this cycle healthy. Investors put in money; the firm invests it; operations generate cash; that cash is either reinvested (if good projects exist) or returned to investors. Understanding this cycle is the foundation of everything else.
The Cycle (4 steps)
Step 1 — Raise capital: investors provide funds (equity or debt)
Step 2 — Invest: firm buys real assets (machines, R&D, acquisitions)
Step 3 — Operate: assets generate revenue → operating cash flow
Step 4a — Reinvest: if ROI > hurdle rate, keep cash in the firm
Step 4b — Return: pay dividends or buy back shares
🔢 How to determine the Hurdle Rate (step by step)
1
Identify project risk: Is this riskier than the firm's average project? Riskier → higher rate.
2
Find the risk-free rate rf: Use the current government bond yield (e.g. 10-yr Bund = 3%).
3
Add a risk premium: Hurdle Rate = rf + risk premium. For an average-risk project, use WACC (see Module 7). e.g. 3% + 7% = 10%
4
Decision rule: If expected project return ≥ hurdle rate → invest. If < hurdle rate → reject.

⚠️ The Agency Problem
Why this matters: When ownership and management are separated (as in corporations), managers may act in their own interests rather than shareholders'. This creates costs — both direct (monitoring expenses) and indirect (missed opportunities). Understanding how to control this conflict is central to Corporate Governance.

The agency problem is the conflict that arises because principals (shareholders) delegate decisions to agents (managers), who have different incentives and more information.

Agency Cost = Monitoring Costs + Bonding Costs + Residual Loss
Monitoring costs: board of directors, auditors, lender covenants — costs paid to watch managers
Bonding costs: management contracts, incentive schemes — costs to align interests
Residual loss: value destroyed despite monitoring — unavoidable remainder
Concrete example: CEO invests €50m in a prestige acquisition with NPV = −€20m (bad for shareholders, good for CEO's profile). Shareholders lose €20m → that is the agency cost.
6 Mechanisms to Control the Agency Problem
#MechanismHow it reduces the problem
1Board of DirectorsDirectly oversees management, can fire the CEO
2AuditorsEnforce GAAP, detect accounting manipulation
3Lenders / CovenantsMonitor cash flows; covenants restrict risky behaviour
4Shareholders (Wall Street Walk)Sell shares if dissatisfied → stock price falls → pressure on CEO
5Hostile Takeover ThreatPoorly managed firms are acquired; bad managers replaced
6Stock Options / Incentive PayTie manager wealth to share price → align interests
❓ Exam Questions – Module 1
What is the difference between profit maximisation and shareholder value maximisation?▼
Profit maximisation ignores the time value of money (€1 today ≠ €1 in 5 years) and risk. Shareholder value = present value of all future cash flows → accounts for time value and risk. A project can generate short-term accounting profit but destroy long-term value (NPV < 0). Correct goal: maximise the present value of future cash flows.
Why do agency costs arise specifically in large corporations?▼
In corporations, ownership (shareholders) and control (management) are separated. Shareholders are often anonymous and lack time/expertise for direct oversight. Managers have an information advantage (asymmetric information). In a sole proprietorship, owner = manager → no conflict. Agency costs include: monitoring costs, bonding costs, and residual loss.
What is the hurdle rate and how is it determined?▼
The hurdle rate is the minimum return a project must generate to be worthwhile. It equals the opportunity cost of capital — what investors could earn elsewhere at the same risk level. Determined via CAPM: r = rf + β(rm−rf), or via WACC for the whole firm. Projects with return > hurdle rate create value (NPV > 0).

Returns · Compounding · Net Present Value · Perpetuity · Annuity

📈 Returns — How We Measure Investment Performance
Why we need this: Before we can evaluate any investment, we need a standardised way to measure what it earns. Returns let us compare a stock, bond, or project on equal footing — regardless of how much money was invested.
Simple Return (one period)
rt = Pt − Pt−1 + DIVtPt−1
Pt = price today  |  Pt−1 = price yesterday  |  DIVt = dividend received
🔢 Step-by-step: Simple Return
1
Identify what you received: You bought a stock for €100, it is now worth €120, and paid a €10 dividend.
2
Capital gain: Pt − Pt−1 = 120 − 100 = €20
3
Total gain: 20 + 10 (dividend) = €30
4
Divide by what you paid: 30 / 100 = 0.30 = 30% ← your return
Geometric (Holding Period) Return — multiple periods
(1+H) = (1+r1)·(1+r2)·····(1+rN)
Annualised: r' = (1+H)1/N − 1
🔢 Step-by-step: Geometric Return (3 years)
1
Year-by-year returns: Year 1: +20%, Year 2: −10%, Year 3: +15%
2
Convert to growth factors: (1.20) × (0.90) × (1.15)
3
Multiply: 1.20 × 0.90 = 1.08  →  1.08 × 1.15 = 1.242 → total growth = +24.2%
4
Annualise (÷ 3 years): r' = 1.2421/3 − 1 = 7.52% per year
5
Why not just average? Arithmetic average = (20−10+15)/3 = 8.33% — this overstates true performance because losses hurt more than gains help.
Use Arithmetic Mean when:
Forecasting the expected return for a single future period. It represents the average outcome you'd expect if you repeated the investment many times independently.
Use Geometric Mean when:
Evaluating how an investment actually performed over time. It is the true compounded annual growth rate (CAGR) earned.
⏱ Compounding — How Frequency Affects Growth
Why we need this: Interest can be paid once a year or every second (continuously). The more often it compounds, the more you earn. This matters hugely for bonds, loans, and any multi-period investment calculation.
General Compounding Formula
FV = PV ·  (1 + r/m)m·t  
m = number of compounding periods per year  |  t = years  |  r = annual rate
🔢 Step-by-step: €100 at r = 10% after 1 year, different frequencies
1
Annual (m=1): 100 × (1 + 0.10/1)¹ = 100 × 1.10 = €110.00 → EAR = 10.00%
2
Semi-annual (m=2): 100 × (1 + 0.05)² = 100 × 1.1025 = €110.25 → EAR = 10.25%
3
Monthly (m=12): 100 × (1 + 0.10/12)¹² ≈ €110.47 → EAR = 10.47%
4
Continuous (m→∞): 100 × e0.10×1 = 100 × 1.10517 = €110.52 → EAR = 10.52%
5
Rule: More frequent compounding → higher effective rate. Continuous compounding gives the theoretical maximum.
💰 Net Present Value (NPV) — The Core Tool of Corporate Finance
Why we need this: A euro today is worth more than a euro next year — because you can invest it and earn a return. NPV converts all future cash flows of a project back to today's value, then subtracts what the project costs. A positive NPV means the project creates wealth; a negative NPV destroys it.
Net Present Value
NPV = −I0 + CF1(1+r)1 + CF2(1+r)2 + ··· + CFn(1+r)n
−I0 = initial investment (negative, cash goes out)  |  CFt = cash flow in year t  |  r = discount rate (risk-adjusted)
🔢 Step-by-step: Full NPV Calculation (r = 10%)
1
List all cash flows: Year 0: −€1,000 (investment) · Year 1: +€300 · Year 2: +€400 · Year 3: +€500 · Year 4: +€600
2
Calculate the discount factor for each year: DFt = 1/(1+0.10)t
Year 1: 1/1.10 = 0.9091  |  Year 2: 1/1.21 = 0.8264  |  Year 3: 1/1.331 = 0.7513  |  Year 4: 1/1.464 = 0.6830
3
Multiply each CF by its discount factor (= Present Value):
PV₁ = 300 × 0.9091 = €272.73  |  PV₂ = 400 × 0.8264 = €330.58
PV₃ = 500 × 0.7513 = €375.66  |  PV₄ = 600 × 0.6830 = €409.81
4
Sum all present values: 272.73 + 330.58 + 375.66 + 409.81 = €1,388.78
5
Subtract the initial investment: NPV = 1,388.78 − 1,000 = +€388.78
6
Decision: NPV > 0 → project creates €389 of value → Accept!
Year tCash Flow× Discount Factor (10%)= Present Value
0−€1,0001.0000−€1,000.00

NPV+€388.78 ✓
NPV Decision Rule: NPV > 0 → creates value → Accept.   NPV = 0 → breaks even → Indifferent.   NPV < 0 → destroys value → Reject.
📐 Special Cash-Flow Patterns: Perpetuity & Annuity
Why we need this: Many real-world cash flows follow predictable patterns — a bond pays the same coupon every year, a stock may grow dividends at a constant rate. These shortcuts let us value them without discounting each payment individually.
Perpetuity — same CF forever
PV = CFr
🔢 Step-by-step: Perpetuity
1
A government bond pays €80 per year forever. Discount rate r = 4%.
2
Apply formula: PV = 80 / 0.04 = €2,000
3
Intuition: If you invest €2,000 at 4%, you earn €80/year → the perpetuity is worth exactly that.
Growing Perpetuity — CF grows at rate g forever
PV = CF1r − g  (requires r > g)
🔢 Step-by-step: Growing Perpetuity (Gordon Growth Model for stocks)
1
A stock pays DIV₁ = €3 next year, dividends grow at g = 5% per year, r = 10%.
2
Denominator: r − g = 10% − 5% = 5%
3
Apply formula: PV = 3 / 0.05 = €60 ← fair price of the stock today
4
Warning: If g ≥ r, the formula breaks (negative or infinite value) → only valid when growth is below the discount rate.
Annuity — fixed CF for exactly T periods
PV = CF · 1 − (1+r)−Tr
🔢 Step-by-step: Annuity (e.g. loan repayment)
1
A project generates €30,000/year for 5 years. Discount rate r = 8%.
2
Calculate the annuity factor: [1 − (1.08)−5] / 0.08 = [1 − 0.6806] / 0.08 = 0.3194 / 0.08 = 3.9927
3
Multiply: 30,000 × 3.9927 = €119,782 ← present value of all 5 payments
4
If the project costs €100,000: NPV = 119,782 − 100,000 = +€19,782 → Accept!
❓ Exam Questions – Module 2
When do you use arithmetic vs. geometric mean?▼
Arithmetic mean: sum of returns / N. Use for forecasting one-period expected return. Geometric mean: (end value/start value)^(1/N) − 1. Use for measuring historical compounded performance. Rule of thumb: Geo ≈ Arith − σ²/2. Higher volatility → bigger gap. Example: Arith = 10%, σ = 20% → Geo ≈ 10% − 0.02 = 8%.
A project costs €100,000 and generates €30,000/year for 5 years at r = 8%. Is it worthwhile?▼
PV = 30,000 × [1−1/(1.08)⁵]/0.08 = 30,000 × 3.9927 = €119,782. NPV = 119,782 − 100,000 = +€19,782 > 0 → Accept. IRR ≈ 15.2% > 8% confirms the result.
Why does NPV use the opportunity cost of capital as the discount rate?▼
The discount rate represents what investors could earn elsewhere at the same risk level. If the project earns less than that, investors are better off investing in the market. The NPV rule says: only invest if the project beats the best available alternative of equal risk. The debt rate is wrong because it ignores the operating risk borne by equity holders.

Bonds & Stocks

Bond valuation · YTM · Duration · Yield Curve · DDM · PVGO

📋 Bond Valuation — What is a Bond Worth Today?
Why we need this: A bond is a promise to pay fixed cash flows in the future. To know its fair price today, we discount those cash flows back using the market's required return. This is the same NPV logic — but applied to a fixed-income security.
Bond Price Formula
P = Σ Coupon(1+r)t + Face Value(1+r)T
Coupon = annual interest payment  |  r = market (discount) rate = YTM  |  T = years to maturity
🔢 Step-by-step: Price a bond (6% coupon, €1,000 face value, 4 years, r = 8%)
1
Annual coupon payment: 6% × €1,000 = €60 per year
2
Discount factor for each year: 1/(1.08)t
Year 1: 0.9259  |  Year 2: 0.8573  |  Year 3: 0.7938  |  Year 4: 0.7350
3
PV of each coupon: 60 × 0.9259 = 55.56  |  60 × 0.8573 = 51.44  |  60 × 0.7938 = 47.63
4
PV of final coupon + face value: 1,060 × 0.7350 = €779.10
5
Sum all PVs: 55.56 + 51.44 + 47.63 + 779.10 = €933.73
6
Why below €1,000? Market rate (8%) > coupon (6%) → new bonds offer more → this bond is less attractive → price falls until buyers earn 8%.
Golden Rule (always exam-relevant): Interest rates ↑ → bond prices ↓.  Interest rates ↓ → bond prices ↑.  Coupon > r → price above par.  Coupon < r → price below par.  Coupon = r → price = par (€1,000).
📏 Yield to Maturity (YTM) — The Bond's True Annual Return
Why we need this: A bond's coupon rate is fixed, but the market price changes daily. YTM tells you the actual annual return you'll earn if you buy the bond now and hold it to maturity. It is the IRR of the bond's cash flows.
YTM: solve for r so that NPV = 0
P = Coupon(1+YTM)1 + Coupon(1+YTM)2 + ··· + Coupon + Face Value(1+YTM)T
🔢 Step-by-step: Find YTM (buy bond for €107.50, coupon €5/year, 5 years, face value €100)
1
Set up the equation: 107.50 = 5/(1+r)¹ + 5/(1+r)² + 5/(1+r)³ + 5/(1+r)⁴ + 105/(1+r)⁵
2
This cannot be solved analytically — you need trial & error or Excel's RATE/IRR function.
3
Try r = 3.34%: Discounting gives PV ≈ €107.50 ✓ → YTM = 3.34% per year
4
Why is YTM (3.34%) < coupon rate (5%)? Because we paid above par (€107.50 > €100). We overpaid, so our actual return is lower than the coupon rate.
5
Rule: Price > par → YTM < coupon.   Price < par → YTM > coupon.   Price = par → YTM = coupon.
📏 Duration — How Sensitive is a Bond to Rate Changes?
Why we need this: Two bonds can have the same maturity but react differently to interest rate changes. Duration tells us how long it takes, on average, to receive the bond's cash flows — and more importantly, by how much the price changes when rates move. Portfolio managers use it to control interest rate risk.
Macaulay Duration (weighted average time of cash flows)
D = Σ t · PV(CFt)P
Modified Duration → % price change per 1% rate move
Dmod = D1 + YTM    then:    ΔPP ≈ −Dmod × ΔYield
🔢 Step-by-step: Duration for a 3-year bond (5% coupon, YTM = 4%)
1
List cash flows and discount them at 4%:
Year 1: CF = €5 → PV = 5/1.04 = €4.808
Year 2: CF = €5 → PV = 5/1.04² = €4.623
Year 3: CF = €105 → PV = 105/1.04³ = €93.35
2
Total price (sum of PVs): 4.808 + 4.623 + 93.35 = P = €102.78
3
Weight each PV by its share of total price:
w₁ = 4.808/102.78 = 0.0468  |  w₂ = 4.623/102.78 = 0.0450  |  w₃ = 93.35/102.78 = 0.9082
4
Multiply each weight by its year: D = 1×0.0468 + 2×0.0450 + 3×0.9082 = 0.047 + 0.090 + 2.725 = D = 2.86 years
5
Modified Duration: Dmod = 2.86 / (1 + 0.04) = 2.75
6
What happens if rates rise by 1%? ΔP/P ≈ −2.75 × 0.01 = −2.75% → price falls from €102.78 to ~€99.95
Duration is higher when:
→ Longer maturity (more payments in the future)
→ Lower coupon (face value carries more weight)
→ Zero-coupon bond: D = maturity exactly
→ Lower YTM (future CFs discounted less)
Duration as a risk shield:
Match portfolio duration to your investment horizon → losses from price drops are exactly offset by higher reinvestment income → "immunisation"
📈 Yield Curve — Why Long-Term Rates Differ from Short-Term
Why we need this: The yield curve plots interest rates vs. maturity for similar bonds (e.g. government bonds). Its shape tells us what markets expect about future growth and inflation. It also determines the correct discount rate for each year of a multi-year project.
🔢 Step-by-step: Pricing with different spot rates (term structure)
1
Bond pays €1 in Year 1 and €1 in Year 2. Spot rates: r₁ = 3%, r₂ = 4% (different rates for different maturities).
2
Discount each payment at its own rate: PV₁ = 1/1.03 = 0.9709  |  PV₂ = 1/(1.04)² = 0.9246
3
Total price: 0.9709 + 0.9246 = €1.8955
4
YTM (single equivalent rate): Solve 1.8955 = 1/(1+y) + 1/(1+y)² → y ≈ 3.66% (average of the two spot rates)

Normal yield curve (upward sloping): long-term rates > short-term → growth expected, liquidity premium.  Inverted: short-term > long-term → recession signal.  Flat: uncertainty, transition.

📊 Stock Valuation — Dividend Discount Model (DDM)
Why we need this: A stock has no maturity date and no fixed payments. Its value comes entirely from future dividends (and the eventual sale price). The DDM applies the NPV logic: discount all expected future cash flows to find the fair price today.
One-period model
P0 = DIV1 + P11 + r
Gordon Growth Model (constant dividend growth g forever)
P0 = DIV1r − g     and rearranged:     r = DIV1P0 + g
Sustainable Growth Rate
g = Plowback Ratio × ROE    where Plowback = 1 − Payout Ratio
🔢 Step-by-step: Gordon Growth Model
1
Given: EPS = €8.33, payout ratio = 60%, ROE = 25%, r = 15%.
2
Plowback ratio: 1 − 0.60 = 0.40 (40% of earnings reinvested)
3
Growth rate g: 0.40 × 25% = 10% per year
4
Next dividend: DIV₁ = EPS × payout = 8.33 × 0.60 = €5.00
5
Stock price: P₀ = 5.00 / (0.15 − 0.10) = 5.00 / 0.05 = €100
6
Compare: no reinvestment (g=0): P₀ = EPS/r = 8.33/0.15 = €55.56
7
PVGO = 100 − 55.56 = €44.44 — the value created purely by reinvesting earnings at ROE > r.
When does reinvesting pay off? Only when ROE > r (the firm earns more on reinvested capital than shareholders demand). If ROE = r → PVGO = 0 (indifferent). If ROE < r → pay out as dividends (shareholders can invest better elsewhere).
❓ Exam Questions – Module 3
Explain the inverse relationship between interest rates and bond prices.▼
A bond pays fixed coupons (e.g. €60/year). When market rates rise, newly issued bonds offer higher coupons. The old bond becomes less attractive → investors only buy it at a lower price, until its YTM matches the market rate. Mathematically: P = Σ CF/(1+r)^t → higher r → larger denominator → smaller present value. Long-duration bonds are more sensitive because more payments are far in the future.
What is Modified Duration and how do you use it?▼
Dmod = Macaulay Duration / (1 + YTM). It gives the percentage change in bond price for a 1% change in yield: ΔP/P ≈ −Dmod × ΔYield. Example: Dmod = 4.5, yield rises 0.5% → price falls ≈ 4.5 × 0.5% = 2.25%. Used by portfolio managers to hedge interest rate risk — match portfolio duration to investment horizon.
When should a firm retain earnings rather than pay dividends?▼
Retention adds value only when the firm can reinvest at ROE > r (shareholders' required return). Then PVGO > 0 → stock price > EPS/r. If ROE = r → indifferent. If ROE < r (e.g. a mature firm with no good projects) → pay dividends so shareholders can redeploy the capital more efficiently elsewhere.
Module 4

Investment Decisions: NPV, IRR & PI

IRR definition · 4 Pitfalls · 4 NPV Rules · Capital Rationing · EAC

⚔️ NPV vs. IRR — Two Ways to Evaluate a Project
Why we need this: NPV tells you the absolute value a project creates (in euros). IRR tells you its percentage return. They usually agree — but in several important cases, IRR gives the wrong answer. Knowing when IRR fails could save you from picking the wrong project.
NPV — always use this
• Gives the value added in € today
• Additive: NPV(A+B) = NPV(A)+NPV(B)
• Always one unique answer
• Accounts for the scale of the investment
IRR — use with caution
• Discount rate where NPV = 0
• Expressed as a % (relative)
• 4 situations where it gives wrong answers
• Assumes reinvestment at the IRR
IRR Definition — solve for r such that NPV = 0
0 = −I0 + CF1(1+IRR) + CF2(1+IRR)2 + ··· + CFn(1+IRR)n
→ Cannot be solved analytically when T > 2. Use Excel IRR() or trial & error.
🔢 Step-by-step: Find IRR (invest −€1,000, receive +€1,200 in Year 1)
1
Set NPV = 0: 0 = −1,000 + 1,200/(1+IRR)
2
Rearrange: 1+IRR = 1,200/1,000 = 1.20
3
IRR = 0.20 = 20% — this project returns 20% per year
4
Decision rule: If IRR (20%) > cost of capital (e.g. 10%) → Accept. Equivalent to NPV > 0 for normal projects.
🚨 The 4 Pitfalls of IRR — When Not to Use It
Pitfall 1 — Lending vs. Borrowing (non-conventional cash flows)
When does this happen? When the first cash flow is positive (you receive money first, then pay it back — like a loan you take out).
Problem: For these cash flows, NPV rises as the discount rate rises — the opposite of normal. The IRR rule is reversed: Accept if IRR < r (not > r).
Example: CF = +1,000 (now), −1,200 (Year 1). IRR = 20%. If r = 15%: NPV = +1,000 − 1,200/1.15 = −€43 < 0. IRR > r would say "Accept" but NPV says "Reject". NPV is correct.
Pitfall 2 — Multiple IRRs
When does this happen? When the cash flow stream changes sign more than once (e.g. −, +, −, + or +, −, +).
Problem: Multiple values of r make NPV = 0 — which IRR do you compare to the hurdle rate?
Example: CF = −100, +300, −250: there are two IRRs (≈3.5% and ≈19.5%). NPV rule has no such ambiguity → always use NPV.
Pitfall 3 — Scale Problem (most important for exams!)
ProjectInvestmentCF Year 1IRRNPV (r=10%)
A (small)−€10,000+€20,000100%+€8,182
B (large)−€20,000+€35,00075%+€11,818
IRR picks A (100% > 75%). But B creates more total wealth (+€11,818 vs +€8,182). If you can only pick one → always choose by NPV.
Pitfall 4 — Changing Discount Rates (Term Structure)
When does this happen? When the correct discount rate changes over time (different rates for Year 1, Year 2, etc.).
Problem: IRR produces a single rate and compares it to a single hurdle rate — but which year's rate do you use?
Solution: NPV discounts each cash flow at the maturity-matched spot rate → no ambiguity.
📜 The 4 NPV Rules — Getting the Cash Flows Right
Why we need this: The NPV formula is simple, but only gives the right answer if you plug in the right cash flows. These four rules tell you exactly what to include and what to leave out. Violating any one of them gives a wrong investment decision.
Rule 1 — Only actual cash flows count (not accounting profit)
!
Formula: Free Cash Flow = Net Income + Depreciation − CAPEX − Δ Net Working Capital
✓
Include: taxes, salvage value, opportunity costs (what you forgo by doing this project)
✗
Exclude sunk costs: money already spent that you cannot recover — e.g. €100k market study done last year. It's gone whether you do the project or not.
✗
Exclude non-cash items: Depreciation reduces accounting profit but is NOT a cash outflow. Add it back.
Rule 2 — Use incremental cash flows (changes caused by the project)
!
Only count cash flows that are different because of the project. Ask: "What changes if we do this project vs. not doing it?"
✓
Include: side effects on other products, cannibalization effects, changes in working capital
✗
Exclude: allocated overhead costs that would exist anyway regardless of the project
Rule 3 — Be consistent with inflation
!
Two valid approaches — but never mix them:
A
Nominal approach: Use nominal cash flows (including inflation) discounted at the nominal rate. r_nominal ≈ r_real + π
B
Real approach: Use real cash flows (inflation stripped out) discounted at the real rate. r_real = (1+r_nom)/(1+π) − 1
✓
Both give the same NPV. Mixing them (real CFs with nominal rate, or vice versa) gives a wrong answer.
Rule 4 — Separate investment and financing decisions
!
Evaluate the project as if it were 100% equity-financed. Financing effects (interest tax shields) are captured separately in the discount rate (WACC).
✗
Do NOT subtract loan proceeds from the initial investment.
✗
Do NOT count interest or debt repayments as project cash outflows.
📊 Capital Rationing & Profitability Index (PI)
Why we need this: With unlimited capital, accept every project with NPV > 0. But when the budget is limited, we need to rank projects by bang-per-buck. The Profitability Index does exactly that.
Profitability Index
PI = NPVRequired Investment
🔢 Step-by-step: Capital Rationing with budget = €300,000
1
Available projects: A (invest €200k, NPV €70k, PI=0.35)  |  B (invest €150k, NPV €45k, PI=0.30)  |  C (invest €100k, NPV €40k, PI=0.40)
2
Rank by PI: C (0.40) > A (0.35) > B (0.30)
3
Fill budget greedily: Take C (€100k left: €300k−€100k=€200k) → Take A (€200k left: €200k−€200k=€0k). Total NPV = €70k + €40k = €110k
4
Compare: if we took A+B: €200k + €150k = €350k > budget → infeasible. C+B = €100k+€150k = €250k ≤ budget, NPV = €85k < €110k.
⚖️ EAC — Comparing Machines with Different Lifespans
Why we need this: Should you buy Machine A (lasts 3 years) or Machine B (lasts 5 years)? Direct NPV comparison is unfair — A covers fewer years. EAC converts the total cost into an equal annual payment, making them comparable.
Equivalent Annual Cost
EAC = NPV(costs)Annuity Factor   where Annuity Factor = 1 − (1+r)−Tr
🔢 Step-by-step: EAC comparison (r = 10%)
1
Machine A: NPV of costs = €50,000 over 3 years. Annuity factor = [1−1/(1.10)³]/0.10 = 2.487
2
EAC(A): 50,000 / 2.487 = €20,104 per year
3
Machine B: NPV of costs = €75,000 over 5 years. Annuity factor = [1−1/(1.10)⁵]/0.10 = 3.791
4
EAC(B): 75,000 / 3.791 = €19,785 per year
5
Decision: EAC(B) < EAC(A) → Machine B is cheaper per year → Choose B.
❓ Exam Questions – Module 4
Why is NPV always superior to IRR?▼
NPV measures absolute value in €, is additive (can combine projects), always has one unique answer, accounts for investment scale, and handles term structures of interest rates correctly. IRR: relative %, can have multiple values, ignores scale, assumes flat yield curve, assumes reinvestment at the IRR. The only case IRR gives the same answer as NPV: normal CF pattern (invest first, then receive), no mutual exclusion, flat yield curve.
What is a sunk cost and why does it NOT belong in the NPV calculation?▼
A sunk cost is money already spent that cannot be recovered — e.g. €100k spent on market research last year. NPV is about incremental cash flows: "what changes if we do the project?" The sunk cost is gone whether we proceed or not → it doesn't change → don't include it. The opposite: opportunity costs MUST be included — if using a factory space for Project A means you can't rent it out for €50k/year, that €50k is a real cost of Project A.
When do you use EAC instead of direct NPV comparison?▼
Use EAC when machines have different useful lives and are replicable (replaced by identical machines at end of life). Direct NPV comparison is misleading because Machine A (3 years) provides 3 years of service while Machine B (5 years) provides 5 years. EAC converts both to cost-per-year → fair comparison. For one-off, non-replicable projects → use direct NPV.
Module 5

Risk & Return

Expected Return · Variance · Portfolio Risk · Beta · Systematic vs. Unsystematic

📊 Expected Return — What Do We Expect on Average?
Why we need this: Investments don't have guaranteed outcomes. The expected return is our best estimate of the average outcome, weighted by how likely each scenario is. It is the foundation for every risk-return calculation.
Expected Return (probability-weighted average)
E[r] = Σ p(s) · r(s)
p(s) = probability of scenario s  |  r(s) = return in scenario s  |  Σp(s) = 1
🔢 Step-by-step: Expected Return
1
Three scenarios: Boom (prob 30%, return +30%), Normal (50%, +10%), Recession (20%, −10%)
2
Multiply probability × return for each scenario:
Boom: 0.30 × 30% = 9%  |  Normal: 0.50 × 10% = 5%  |  Recession: 0.20 × (−10%) = −2%
3
Sum them up: 9% + 5% + (−2%) = E[r] = 12%
4
Interpretation: On average, if this scenario plays out many times, you'd earn 12% per period.
📐 Variance & Standard Deviation — How Risky is the Investment?
Why we need this: A high expected return is worthless if the actual outcome could be wildly different. Variance measures the spread of possible outcomes around the expected return. Standard deviation (= √variance) is the most common risk measure — it is in the same units as the return (%).
Variance (ex-ante — using probabilities)
σ² = Σ p(s) · [r(s) − E[r]]²
Variance (ex-post — using historical data)
σ² = Σ(rt − r̄)²T − 1  (T−1 = Bessel correction for sample)
Standard Deviation (Volatility)
σ = √σ²
🔢 Step-by-step: Variance (continuing the example, E[r] = 12%)
1
Deviation from mean for each scenario:
Boom: 30% − 12% = +18%  |  Normal: 10% − 12% = −2%  |  Recession: −10% − 12% = −22%
2
Square each deviation: (0.18)² = 0.0324  |  (−0.02)² = 0.0004  |  (−0.22)² = 0.0484
3
Multiply by probability and sum:
0.30×0.0324 + 0.50×0.0004 + 0.20×0.0484 = 0.00972 + 0.00020 + 0.00968 = σ² = 0.0196
4
Standard deviation: σ = √0.0196 = 14% — the return typically deviates ±14% from the 12% expected return.

Boom0.30+30%+9%+18%0.00972

📐 Portfolio Return & Variance — How Two Stocks Combine
Why we need this: Combining two stocks often creates a portfolio with lower risk than either stock alone. This is diversification. The portfolio variance formula tells us exactly how much risk reduction we get, depending on how the stocks move together (correlation).
Portfolio Return (weighted average)
rp = x1·r1 + x2·r2  (x₁ + x₂ = 1)
Portfolio Variance — the key formula
σ²p = x1²σ1² + x2²σ2² + 2·x1·x2·ρ12·σ1·σ2
ρ12 = correlation coefficient (−1 to +1)  |  Cov(1,2) = ρ12·σ1·σ2
🔢 Step-by-step: Portfolio variance (60% Stock 1, 40% Stock 2)
1
Given: x₁=0.60, x₂=0.40, σ₁=20%, σ₂=15%, ρ = 0.3
2
Term 1 (Stock 1 alone): x₁²·σ₁² = (0.60)²·(0.20)² = 0.36 × 0.04 = 0.0144
3
Term 2 (Stock 2 alone): x₂²·σ₂² = (0.40)²·(0.15)² = 0.16 × 0.0225 = 0.0036
4
Term 3 (interaction): 2·x₁·x₂·ρ·σ₁·σ₂ = 2·0.60·0.40·0.30·0.20·0.15 = 0.00432
5
σ²p = 0.0144 + 0.0036 + 0.00432 = 0.02232 → σp = √0.02232 = 14.94%
6
If ρ = −1: Term 3 = 2·0.60·0.40·(−1)·0.20·0.15 = −0.0144. σ²p = 0.0144+0.0036−0.0144 = 0.0036 → σp = 6% — massive risk reduction!
Diversification key fact: Lower ρ → more risk reduction. At ρ = +1: no diversification (risks add linearly). At ρ = 0: moderate benefit. At ρ = −1: maximum diversification (can get σp = 0 with the right weights).
🎯 Systematic vs. Unsystematic Risk
Why we need this: Not all risk is equal. Risk that is specific to one company (e.g. a bad CEO, a factory fire) can be eliminated by holding many stocks. Risk that affects the whole market (recession, interest rate changes) cannot. Only market risk deserves a risk premium — you are not rewarded for taking avoidable risk.

📏 Beta (β) — Measure of Systematic Risk
Why we need this: Beta tells us how much a stock moves relative to the overall market. A stock with β = 2 rises (and falls) twice as much as the market. This determines how much risk premium the stock should earn — and feeds directly into the CAPM (Module 7).
Beta
βi = Cov(ri, rm)σ²m = ρim · σi · σmσ²m = ρim · σiσm
🔢 Step-by-step: Calculate Beta
1
Given: Cov(stock, market) = 0.018, σ²market = 0.04 (i.e. σm = 20%)
2
β = Cov/σ²m = 0.018 / 0.04 = 0.45
3
Interpretation: When the market moves +10%, this stock moves +4.5% on average. It is less volatile than the market → defensive stock (e.g. utility company).
4
Portfolio Beta: βp = x₁·β₁ + x₂·β₂ + … (weighted average — Beta is additive!)
β > 1: more volatile than market (tech stocks)  |  β = 1: moves with market  |  β < 1: defensive (utilities, staples)  |  β < 0: moves opposite (rare — e.g. gold)
❓ Exam Questions – Module 5
Why is unsystematic risk not compensated with a risk premium?▼
Unsystematic risk can be eliminated for free through diversification — just hold many different stocks. Since rational investors do this, the market will not pay a premium for risk you chose to keep. Market prices reflect only non-diversifiable systematic risk. A poorly diversified investor bears extra risk but earns no extra return — they are simply making a mistake.
What happens to portfolio variance as you add more and more stocks?▼
With N stocks equally weighted: σ²p = (1/N)·σ̄²i + (1−1/N)·Cov̄. As N→∞: the individual variance term (1/N)→0. σ²p → Cov̄ (average covariance) = systematic risk. Unsystematic risk fully disappears. In practice: with ~20–30 carefully chosen stocks, ~90% of diversifiable risk is already eliminated.
Stock A: E[r]=15%, σ=25%. Stock B: E[r]=12%, σ=18%. Which is better?▼
Cannot answer without the Sharpe Ratio. At rf=4%: Sharpe(A) = (15−4)/25 = 0.44. Sharpe(B) = (12−4)/18 = 0.44. Identical risk-adjusted performance! A risk-averse investor prefers B (same ratio, lower volatility). In a portfolio context, what matters is each stock's correlation with the rest — not σ alone.
Module 6

Portfolio Theory & Efficient Frontier

Mean-Variance Optimisation · Efficient Frontier · CAL · Tangency Portfolio · Sharpe Ratio

🎯 Mean-Variance Optimisation — Finding the Best Portfolio
Why we need this: With many stocks to choose from, we need a systematic way to build the best possible portfolio. Mean-variance theory finds the portfolios that give the highest expected return for any given level of risk — or the lowest risk for any target return. Every rational investor should hold one of these efficient portfolios.

Core idea: There is a trade-off between risk (variance) and return (mean). The goal is to be on the efficient frontier — the set of portfolios where no better risk-return combination exists.

🔑 Key Building Blocks: Covariance & Correlation
Why we need this: Two stocks can have the same individual risks, but their combination may be much less risky — if they don't move together. Covariance and correlation measure exactly how much they move together.
Covariance — measures joint movement
Cov(r1, r2) = Σ p(s) · [r1(s) − E[r1]] · [r2(s) − E[r2]]
Correlation — standardised covariance, ranges −1 to +1
ρ12 = Cov(r1, r2)σ1 · σ2
🔢 Step-by-step: Calculate Covariance & Correlation
1
Two assets, two scenarios (50/50): Scenario A: r₁=+10%, r₂=+8%. Scenario B: r₁=−2%, r₂=+4%.
2
Expected returns: E[r₁] = 0.5×10% + 0.5×(−2%) = 4%  |  E[r₂] = 0.5×8% + 0.5×4% = 6%
3
Deviations: Scenario A: (10%−4%)=+6%, (8%−6%)=+2%. Scenario B: (−2%−4%)=−6%, (4%−6%)=−2%
4
Cov: 0.5×(+6%×+2%) + 0.5×(−6%×−2%) = 0.5×0.12% + 0.5×0.12% = 0.12% (positive → move together)
5
σ₁: √[0.5×(6%)²+0.5×(6%)²] = 6%  |  σ₂: √[0.5×(2%)²+0.5×(2%)²] = 2%
6
ρ = Cov/(σ₁×σ₂) = 0.12%/(6%×2%) = 0.12%/0.12% = +1.0 → perfectly positively correlated (no diversification benefit here)
📈 The Efficient Frontier — Map of All Good Portfolios
Why we need this: By combining two stocks in different proportions, we get a curve of portfolio outcomes. The efficient frontier is the upper part of this curve — the portfolios with the best return for their level of risk. Any portfolio below it is suboptimal.
🔢 Step-by-step: Building the efficient frontier (2 assets)
1
Asset A: E[r]=10%, σ=20%. Asset B: E[r]=6%, σ=10%. Correlation ρ = 0.2
2
Choose a weight: xA = 50%, xB = 50%
3
Portfolio return: E[rp] = 0.5×10% + 0.5×6% = 8%
4
Portfolio variance: σ²p = (0.5)²(0.20)² + (0.5)²(0.10)² + 2(0.5)(0.5)(0.2)(0.20)(0.10)
= 0.01 + 0.0025 + 0.002 = 0.0145 → σp = 12.04%
5
Repeat for all weights from xA=0% to 100% in steps. The resulting curve is the portfolio frontier.
6
Efficient part: Only the upper half of the curve (from GMVP upward) — lower half has same σ but less return.
GMVP — Global Minimum Variance Portfolio
The single point on the frontier with the lowest possible σ.
Portfolios below GMVP: inefficient (same risk, lower return → never choose these).
Tangency Portfolio T
The portfolio on the efficient frontier with the highest Sharpe Ratio.
Found by drawing a straight line from rf tangent to the frontier.
Under CAPM: T = the Market Portfolio.
📐 Sharpe Ratio — Return per Unit of Risk
Why we need this: The Sharpe Ratio lets us rank portfolios on a risk-adjusted basis. The portfolio with the highest Sharpe Ratio is the one that gives the most return for every unit of risk taken. The Tangency Portfolio maximises the Sharpe Ratio.
Sharpe Ratio = slope of the Capital Allocation Line
SR = E[rp] − rfσp
🔢 Step-by-step: Sharpe Ratio
1
Portfolio A: E[r]=14%, σ=20%, rf=4%
2
SR(A) = (14% − 4%) / 20% = 10%/20% = 0.50
3
Portfolio B: E[r]=11%, σ=14%, rf=4% → SR(B) = 7%/14% = 0.50 — same Sharpe, B has less risk!
4
Which to prefer? Same SR → same risk-adjusted performance. A risk-averse investor prefers B (lower σ). A risk-tolerant investor can leverage B to match A's return.
📐 Capital Allocation Line (CAL)
Why we need this: Once we have the Tangency Portfolio, we can mix it with a risk-free asset to achieve any desired risk level. The CAL is the set of all such mixtures — it is a straight line in risk-return space. Any point on the CAL is achievable and optimal for some level of risk aversion.
Capital Allocation Line
E[rp] = rf + E[rT] − rfσT · σp
Slope = Sharpe Ratio of the Tangency Portfolio  |  rf = y-intercept
Portfolio split: fraction a in T, (1−a) in risk-free
E[rp] = (1−a)·rf + a·E[rT]    σp = a·σT
🔢 Step-by-step: Use the CAL to reach a target risk level
1
Given: rf=4%, Tangency Portfolio: E[rT]=14%, σT=20%. Investor wants σp=12%.
2
Find fraction a: σp = a·σT → a = 12%/20% = 0.60 → invest 60% in T, 40% in risk-free
3
Expected return: E[rp] = 4% + 0.60×(14%−4%) = 4% + 6% = 10%
4
Check via CAL formula: E[rp] = 4% + (10%/20%)×12% = 4% + 6% = 10% ✓
5
If investor wants higher return (a > 1): Borrow money at rf and invest more than 100% in T (leverage).
Two-Fund Separation Theorem: Every rational investor holds the same risky portfolio (the Tangency Portfolio) — only the split between T and the risk-free asset differs based on individual risk tolerance. This is why index funds are theoretically optimal.
❓ Exam Questions – Module 6
What is the efficient frontier and why does the GMVP matter?▼
The efficient frontier is the set of all portfolios with the minimum variance for a given expected return. It is shaped like a parabola in (σ, E[r])-space. The GMVP (Global Minimum Variance Portfolio) is the leftmost point — the portfolio with the absolute lowest risk. Portfolios below GMVP on the same curve are inefficient: same risk, lower return. The efficient portion runs from GMVP upward.
How do you find the Tangency Portfolio?▼
The Tangency Portfolio T is found by drawing a straight line (the CAL) from the risk-free rate rf that is tangent to the efficient frontier. The tangent point is T — the portfolio with the maximum Sharpe Ratio (E[r]−rf)/σ. Under CAPM, all investors agree on T, so T must be the market portfolio.
Given rf=4%, E[rT]=14%, σT=20%. Investor targets E[r]=10%. What is σp?▼
From CAL: E[rp] = rf + SR × σp → 10% = 4% + (10/20) × σp → 6% = 0.5 × σp → σp = 12%. Or: a = (10%−4%)/(14%−4%) = 0.6 → σp = 0.6×20% = 12%. Portfolio: 60% in T, 40% in risk-free.
Module 7

CAPM, WACC & Cost of Capital

Security Market Line · COC · WACC · APT · Fama-French

📐 CAPM — How Much Should a Risky Asset Return?
Why we need this: CAPM answers the single most important question in finance: given a stock's systematic risk (Beta), what return should it offer? This is the fair required return. If a stock offers more → it is undervalued (buy). If less → overvalued (avoid). CAPM is also used to calculate the cost of equity for WACC.
CAPM — Capital Asset Pricing Model
ri = rf + βi · (rm − rf)
rf = risk-free rate (e.g. 10-yr government bond)  |  βi = stock's systematic risk  |  (rm−rf) = market risk premium (≈ 6–8% historically)
🔢 Step-by-step: CAPM calculation
1
Inputs: rf = 3%, market risk premium (rm−rf) = 7%, β = 1.4
2
Risk premium of this stock: β × (rm−rf) = 1.4 × 7% = 9.8%
3
Required return: r = rf + risk premium = 3% + 9.8% = 12.8%
4
Compare to actual expected return: If the stock actually offers E[r] = 15% → it is above the SML → undervalued (Alpha = 15%−12.8% = +2.2%)
5
If E[r] = 11% (below SML): Stock is overvalued (Alpha = −1.8%). Investors avoid it → price falls → yield rises back to 12.8%.
📈 Security Market Line (SML) — The Fair Pricing Map
Why we need this: The SML is a graph with Beta on the x-axis and expected return on the y-axis. Every fairly priced asset lies exactly on this line. Above the SML = cheap (buy). Below = expensive (avoid). This is how analysts identify mispriced stocks.
Above SML → Undervalued
Too high return for the risk → investors buy it → price rises → yield falls → back to SML
Alpha (α) > 0
Below SML → Overvalued
Too low return for the risk → investors sell → price falls → yield rises → back to SML
Alpha (α) < 0

💼 Company Cost of Capital (COC)
Why we need this: A company is financed by both equity and debt. Each has a different cost. The Company Cost of Capital (COC) is the weighted average of these costs — it tells us the minimum return the firm's assets must earn to satisfy all investors.
COC — unlevered (no tax adjustment)
rassets = DV · rdebt + EV · requity
V = D + E (total firm value, market values)  |  D = market value of debt  |  E = market value of equity
🔢 Step-by-step: COC calculation
1
Market cap (E) = €600m, Debt (D) = €400m → V = €1,000m
2
Weights: D/V = 400/1,000 = 0.40  |  E/V = 600/1,000 = 0.60
3
COC (no tax): 0.40 × 5% + 0.60 × 12% = 2.0% + 7.2% = 9.2%
🏗️ WACC — The Most Important Formula in Corporate Finance
Why we need this: WACC is the discount rate used to evaluate any project with the same risk as the existing firm. The key difference from COC: interest payments on debt are tax-deductible (the "tax shield"), which makes debt cheaper than it appears. WACC accounts for this benefit and is therefore lower than the unlevered COC.
WACC — Weighted Average Cost of Capital
WACC = DV·rd·(1−tc) + EV·re
rd = pre-tax cost of debt  |  tc = corporate tax rate  |  re = cost of equity (via CAPM)  |  (1−tc) = tax shield factor
🔢 Step-by-step: Full WACC calculation
1
Inputs: E = €600m, D = €400m, rd = 5%, re = 12%, tc = 30%
2
Weights: D/V = 0.40, E/V = 0.60
3
After-tax cost of debt: rd×(1−tc) = 5%×(1−0.30) = 5%×0.70 = 3.5%
4
Debt contribution: D/V × after-tax rd = 0.40 × 3.5% = 1.4%
5
Equity contribution: E/V × re = 0.60 × 12% = 7.2%
6
WACC = 1.4% + 7.2% = 8.6%
7
Use: For any project with similar risk, accept if expected return ≥ 8.6%. This is the firm's hurdle rate.
⚠️ Always use market values (not book values) for D and E. Book values are historical costs; market values reflect what investors actually think the firm is worth. Also: WACC changes when the capital structure changes — more debt → different WACC.
📊 Estimating re — Cost of Equity via CAPM
Why we need this: To calculate WACC, we need re — what return equity investors demand. CAPM gives us this from the firm's Beta. The challenge: Beta must be estimated from historical data, which is imprecise.
🔢 Step-by-step: Estimate re using CAPM
1
Collect historical data: Monthly stock returns and market returns for the past 5 years.
2
Run OLS regression: rstock = α + β·rmarket + ε → the slope coefficient β̂ is the estimated Beta.
3
Get current rf: Use the 10-year government bond yield (e.g. 3%)
4
Estimate the market risk premium (ERP): Typically use historical average ≈ 6–8%, or an implied ERP from current market prices.
5
Apply CAPM: re = rf + β̂ × ERP. Example: 3% + 1.2 × 7% = 11.4%
6
Caveat: Past Beta ≠ future Beta. The standard error of β̂ can be large → estimate is uncertain. Always sanity-check against industry betas.
🔬 APT & Fama-French — Beyond CAPM
Why we need this: CAPM uses only one risk factor (market Beta). In reality, stock returns are driven by multiple factors. APT and Fama-French extend CAPM to capture more of the variation in returns.
APT — Arbitrage Pricing Theory (multiple factors)
E[ri] = rf + β1·λ1 + β2·λ2 + ··· + βk·λk
λk = risk premium for factor k  |  βk = sensitivity of asset to factor k
Fama-French 3-Factor Model
E[ri] = rf + βi·MKT + si·SMB + hi·HML
MKT = market excess return  |  SMB = small minus big (size)  |  HML = high minus low book-to-market (value)
🔢 How to read Fama-French factors
MKT
Market factor: same as CAPM. If β=1.2 and MKT=7%: contribution = 1.2×7% = 8.4%
SMB
Size factor (Small Minus Big): Small firms historically outperform large firms. If s=0.5 and SMB=3%: contribution = 0.5×3% = 1.5%. Positive s → more like a small-cap stock.
HML
Value factor (High Minus Low): High book-to-market (value stocks) outperform growth stocks historically. If h=0.4 and HML=4%: contribution = 0.4×4% = 1.6%.
Σ
Total re: rf (3%) + 8.4% + 1.5% + 1.6% = 14.5% — higher than CAPM's 3%+8.4% = 11.4% because small-cap and value exposure adds risk premium.

❓ Exam Questions – Module 7
Walk through a full WACC calculation from scratch.▼
Step 1: Get market values of equity E and debt D. V = D + E. Compute D/V and E/V. Step 2: Cost of debt rd = YTM on the firm's bonds. Step 3: Cost of equity re = rf + β × ERP (from CAPM). Step 4: Get tax rate tc. Step 5: WACC = (D/V)×rd×(1−tc) + (E/V)×re. Example: D/V=0.4, rd=5%, tc=30%, E/V=0.6, re=12% → WACC = 0.4×3.5% + 0.6×12% = 1.4%+7.2% = 8.6%.
Why is WACC lower than the unlevered COC?▼
Interest on debt is tax-deductible — the government effectively subsidises part of the borrowing cost. After-tax cost of debt = rd×(1−tc) < rd. This "tax shield" reduces the effective cost of debt. Since WACC uses after-tax debt cost while COC uses pre-tax, WACC < COC when tc > 0 and D > 0. The benefit increases with leverage.
What does it mean if a stock lies above vs. below the Security Market Line?▼
Above SML: the stock offers more expected return than CAPM says it should for its Beta → it is underpriced. Investors buy → price rises → yield falls back to SML. Below SML: stock offers less return than its Beta warrants → overpriced. Investors sell → price falls → yield rises. In equilibrium all stocks lie on the SML — deviations (Alpha) are arbitraged away.
Under what conditions does Tangency Portfolio = Market Portfolio?▼
Under CAPM assumptions: all investors have the same information, same mean-variance preferences, access to the same risk-free asset, and frictionless markets. Under these conditions, all rational investors demand the same Tangency Portfolio. In equilibrium, demand must equal supply → the Tangency Portfolio must be the Market Portfolio (all assets, weighted by market cap). This is why passive index investing is theoretically optimal.
