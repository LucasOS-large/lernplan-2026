Module 1

Introduction to Corporate Finance

Core questions of corporate finance · Goals · Agency Problem

🏢 What is Corporate Finance?

Corporate Finance deals with two core decisions: What should the firm invest in? (Investment decision) and How should it be financed? (Financing decision). The goal is always to maximise firm value for the owners.

INVESTMENT DECISION (CAPEX)
Purchase of real assets (tangible/intangible) → Capital Budgeting.
Example: New factory, patent, IT system.
Decision criterion: NPV > 0 or return > Hurdle Rate.
FINANCING DECISION
Issuance of financial assets → optimise capital structure.
Example: Stock issuance, bonds, bank loan.
Question: How much equity vs. debt? What terms?

💰 Cash Flow Cycle
Investors provide capital → Firm invests in real assets → Operating cash flow is generated → Return to investors (dividends, interest) or reinvestment

The capital market is an efficient mechanism for allocating capital. Hurdle Rate = minimum required return on an investment = opportunity cost of capital. The hurdle rate reflects project risk – higher risk → higher hurdle rate.

⚠️ The Agency Problem

In large corporations, owners (shareholders = principals) and managers (agents) are separated. Managers may pursue their own interests: prestige, job security, empire-building for higher pay – instead of maximising shareholder value. These Agency Costs arise from conflicts of interest.

Example: CEO invests in a prestigious project with NPV < 0 (boosts CEO profile but harms shareholders) → Agency Cost.

❓ Exam Questions – Module 1
What is the difference between profit maximisation and shareholder value maximisation?▼
Profit maximisation ignores the time value of money (€1 today ≠ €1 in 5 years) and risk. Shareholder value = present value of all future cash flows → accounts for time value and risk. A project can generate short-term profit but destroy long-term value (NPV < 0) – e.g. by depleting resources. Correct goal: maximise the present value of future cash flows, not accounting profit.
Why do agency costs arise specifically in corporations?▼
In corporations, ownership (shareholders) and control (management) are separated. Shareholders are often anonymous and lack time/expertise for direct monitoring. Managers have an information advantage (asymmetric information). In a sole proprietorship, owner = manager → no conflict of interest. Agency costs include: monitoring costs (board, auditors), bonding costs (management contracts), residual loss (remaining efficiency loss).
What is the hurdle rate and how is it determined?▼
The hurdle rate is the minimum return an investment must earn to be attractive to investors. It equals the opportunity cost of capital – what investors could alternatively earn in the capital market at the same risk level. Determined via CAPM (r = rf + β(rm−rf)) or WACC. Projects with return > hurdle rate create value (NPV > 0).
What is the difference between real assets and financial assets?▼
Real assets (tangible: buildings, machinery; intangible: patents, brands) are the actually productive assets that generate cash flows. Financial assets (stocks, bonds, derivatives) are claims on the cash flows of real assets – they redistribute value but do not create it. Corporate Finance asks: which real assets are worth it (NPV > 0) and how to finance their purchase?

❓ Exam Questions – Module 2
When do you use arithmetic vs. geometric mean?▼
Arithmetic mean = sum of returns / N. Use for forecasting future returns (expected return in one period). Geometric mean = (end value/start value)^(1/N) − 1. Use for measuring historical performance (actual compounded return earned). Rule of thumb: Geo ≈ Arith. − σ²/2. The higher the volatility, the larger the difference.
Why does the NPV discount rate reflect project risk – not the cost of debt?▼
The discount rate represents the opportunity cost of capital – what investors could alternatively earn at the same risk level. The debt rate is the return for lenders (low risk, senior claim). The project bears the full operating risk of the business. Using the debt rate would make risky projects look too attractive. Correct: use WACC or un-levered cost of capital (for all-equity financing).
A project costs €100,000 and generates €30,000/year for 5 years, r=8%. Is it worthwhile?▼
PV of annuity = 30,000 · [1−1/(1.08)⁵]/0.08 = 30,000 · 3.9927 = 119,782. NPV = 119,782 − 100,000 = +19,782 > 0 → investment is worthwhile. IRR approximation: at r≈18.6%, NPV=0. Since IRR > 8%, this confirms the NPV result.
What is the difference between continuous and discrete compounding?▼
Discrete compounding: interest is credited at specific points in time (annually, semi-annually, etc.), EAR = (1+r/m)^m − 1. Continuous compounding: interest grows instantaneously, A = P·e^(rt). At r=10%: annual discrete → 10.00%, semi-annual → 10.25%, continuous → 10.52%. Continuous compounding is the theoretical maximum EAR. Log returns are the discrete equivalents of continuously compounded returns.

Shows YTM vs. remaining maturity for similar bonds (e.g. government bonds). Normal (upward sloping): Long-term rates > short-term → growth expectations, liquidity premium. PVGO Example (Present Value of Growth Opportunities)

Given: EPS=€5, r=12%, plowback=40%, ROE=25%.
g = 0.40 × 25% = 10%. DIV₁ = EPS × (1−0.40) = €3. Price with growth: P₀ = 3/(0.12−0.10) = €150. Price without growth: EPS/r = 5/0.12 = €41.67. PVGO = 150 − 41.67 = €108.33 → 72% of the stock value comes from growth opportunities!

When does retention pay off? Only when ROE > r (here 25% > 12%) → PVGO > 0. When ROE = r → retention is indifferent. When ROE < r → pay dividends (capital is more efficiently deployed by shareholders).
❓ Exam Questions – Module 3
Explain the inverse relationship between interest rates and bond prices.▼
A bond pays fixed coupons (e.g. €60 p.a.). When market rates rise, new investors demand higher coupons. The old bond with €60 coupon becomes less attractive → its price falls until YTM equals the market rate. Mathematically: P = Σ CF/(1+r)^t → higher r → smaller denominators → smaller present value. The longer the maturity, the more payments are affected → greater price sensitivity (higher duration).
How is Macaulay Duration calculated and what does it measure?▼
D = Σ[t × PV(CF_t)] / P. It is the weighted average time to receive cash flows, where the weights are the PV-share of each cash flow in the total price. As a risk measure: ΔP/P ≈ −ModD × ΔY. Duration-matching (portfolio duration = investment horizon) immunises against interest rate risk – capital losses are offset by reinvestment gains.
When is it beneficial to retain earnings rather than pay dividends?▼
Retention pays off precisely when the firm can reinvest retained earnings at a return (ROE) above the required return r of shareholders. Then PVGO > 0 → stock price > EPS/r. Example: ROE=25%, r=12% → reinvestment is advantageous. When ROE = r → retention is indifferent. When ROE < r (e.g. mature firm with no good projects) → pay dividends.

Investment Decisions: NPV, IRR, PI

The 4 IRR pitfalls · The 4 NPV rules · Capital rationing · EAC

⚔️ NPV vs. IRR – Comparison
NPV – always correct
Absolute value contribution in €
Additive: NPV(A+B) = NPV(A)+NPV(B)
Unique, consistent with market value
Accounts for investment scale
IRR – Caution!
Rate at which NPV = 0
Relative measure (%), not absolute
For normal CFs: IRR > r → invest
Can be misleading (4 pitfalls!)
IRR-Definition (NPV = 0 setzen, nach r auflösen)
0 = −I0 + CF1(1+IRR)1 + CF2(1+IRR)2 + ··· + CFn(1+IRR)n
→ Iterativ lösen (kein analytischer Weg bei T > 2)
🚨 The 4 Pitfalls of IRR
Pitfall 1 – Lending vs. Borrowing (non-conventional CFs):
When CFs are first positive then negative (borrowing pattern), NPV rises with higher discount rates. The IRR rule must be reversed: IRR < r → invest. Recognisable by: first CF positive, then negative.
Pitfall 2 – Multiple IRRs:
When there is more than one sign change in the CF stream, the NPV function can have multiple roots. Example: −100, +300, −250 → two IRRs. NPV rule is unique; IRR is not applicable.
Pitfall 3 – Scale Problem (most important pitfall!):
ProjectInvestmentCF at t=1IRRNPV (r=10%)
A−10.000+20.000100%+8.182 €
B−20.000+35.00075%+11.818 €
IRR rule: choose A (100% > 75%). NPV rule: choose B (+11,818 > +8,182). B is better! IRR ignores scale – a large project with a moderate IRR can create more value.
Pitfall 4 – Term Structure:
IRR is compared against a single discount rate. With a term structure (different rates for different maturities) there is no unique benchmark rate. NPV discounts each CF at the maturity-matched rate.
📜 The 4 NPV Rules
Rule 1 – Only cash flows count (not accounting profit): CF = Net income + Depreciation − CAPEX − ΔNWC → Ignore sunk costs! | Include opportunity costs | Account for taxes & salvage value
Rule 2 – Incremental basis: Only changes caused by the project count. Ignore overhead costs that arise independently of the project.
Rule 3 – Treat inflation consistently: Nominal rate for nominal CFs | Real rate for real CFs Real rate = (1+r_nominal) / (1+π) − 1
Rule 4 – Separate investment from financing: No debt proceeds in CF | No interest payments as project CF Financing effects are captured in the discount rate (WACC).
📊 Capital Rationing & PI
Profitability Index (PI): PI = NPV / Investment amount → With a limited budget: rank by PI until budget is exhausted → Choose the combination that maximises total NPV
PI caveat: PI applies only under a binding capital budget constraint. With unlimited capital, always accept all projects with NPV > 0.
⚖️ EAC – Equivalent Annual Cost
Equivalent Annual Cost (EAC): EAC = NPV(costs) × r / [1 − 1/(1+r)^T] = NPV(costs) / Annuity factor Use: compare machines with different useful lives (Machine A: 3 years vs. Machine B: 5 years). Choose the lower EAC.
❓ Exam Questions – Module 4
Why is NPV generally superior to IRR?▼
NPV measures the absolute value contribution in €, is additive (portfolio view possible), unique (always one solution), and accounts for investment scale. IRR: relative measure (%) ignores scale, can have multiple solutions, assumes a flat yield curve, and implies reinvestment at the IRR (unrealistic when IRR is high). The only situation where IRR is unproblematic: normal CF pattern (− then +), no scale comparison, flat yield curve.
What is a sunk cost and why is it ignored?▼
Sunk cost = an expenditure already made that cannot be recovered (e.g. market research €100,000 before the project). It is irreversible and occurs regardless of whether the project is undertaken. NPV decisions are based on incremental cash flows – what changes because of the project? Since sunk costs do not change, they do not belong in the NPV calculation. Opposite: opportunity costs must be included, as they represent the forgone benefit of the next best alternative.
When do you use EAC instead of a direct NPV comparison?▼
Use EAC when projects have different useful lives and are replicable (machine is replaced by an identical one at end of life). Machine A (3y, NPV costs=€50,000) vs. B (5y, NPV costs=€75,000). Direct NPV comparison is unfair (A covers 3, B covers 5 years). EAC(A) = 50,000 × r/[1−1/(1+r)³]; EAC(B) = 75,000 × r/[1−1/(1+r)⁵] → lower EAC is cheaper. For non-replicable projects (one-off): use direct NPV comparison.

📏 Beta (β) – Measure of Systematic Risk
Beta (β) – measure of systematic risk
βi = Cov(ri, rm)σ2m
β > 1  → more volatile than market (e.g. tech)
β = 1  → moves with the market

Portfolio Beta: β_p = Σ x_i · β_i (weighted average of individual betas). Unlike variance, beta is additive!
📚 Historical Evidence: Risk Premia

Over long horizons (US data 1926–present): stocks ≈ 10–12% p.a., government bonds ≈ 5–6%, money market ≈ 3–4%. Equity Risk Premium (ERP) = stock return − risk-free rate ≈ 6–8% historically. Stocks are compensated for bearing higher risk. In the short run unsystematic risk dominates – only in the long run does the market trend dominate.

❓ Exam Questions – Module 5
Why is unsystematic risk not compensated with a risk premium?▼
Unsystematic risk can be eliminated through diversification – simply hold many different stocks. Since rational investors do this (for free!), they will not pay a premium for risk they can eliminate themselves. Market prices only reflect non-diversifiable systematic risk. A poorly diversified investor bears more risk but receives no higher return – they are simply making a mistake. Conclusion: always hold a well-diversified portfolio.
Stock A: E[r]=15%, σ=25%. Stock B: E[r]=12%, σ=18%. Which do you choose?▼
No clear answer without the Sharpe Ratio! A offers more return (+3%), B less risk (−7%). Sharpe(A) = (15−rf)/25, Sharpe(B) = (12−rf)/18. At rf=4%: Sharpe(A)=(15−4)/25=0.44, Sharpe(B)=(12−4)/18=0.44 → identical! A risk-averse investor prefers B (same return-per-risk ratio). In a portfolio context what matters is the correlation with the rest of the portfolio – not σ alone.
What happens to portfolio variance as N → ∞?▼
With equal weights in N stocks: σ²_p = (1/N)·σ̄²_i + (1−1/N)·Cov̄. As N→∞: (1/N)→0, variances disappear, σ²_p → Cov̄ (average covariance). Unsystematic risk is fully diversified away. Only systematic market risk remains = covariance of all stocks with the market. In practice: with ~20–30 well-chosen stocks, ~90% of diversification is already achieved.
Module 6

Portfolio Theory & Efficient Frontier

Efficient Frontier · GMVP · CAL · Tangency Portfolio · Sharpe Ratio

🎯 Mean-Variance Optimisation

Goal: find the portfolio with the highest expected return at a given level of risk (or the lowest risk at a given return). Assumptions: Frictionless markets, no taxes/transaction costs, perfectly divisible securities, no short-selling restrictions, investors are mean-variance optimal and risk-averse.

Correlation
ρXY = Cov(X,Y)σX·σY  |  ρ ∈ [−1, +1]
Variance of a linear combination
Var(aX+bY) = a2σ2X + b2σ2Y + 2ab·Cov(X,Y)
Sharpe Ratio
SR = E[rp] − rfσp  → return per unit of risk
📈 The Efficient Frontier

The set of all mean-variance efficient portfolios in (σ,E[r])-space. No other portfolio offers higher return at the same risk, or the same risk at higher return.

GMVP (Global Minimum Variance Portfolio)
• Lowest σ on the entire frontier
• Below GMVP: inefficient (same σ, less μ)
• Efficient portion: curve from GMVP upward
Tangency Portfolio T
• Highest Sharpe Ratio of all portfolios
• Point where the CAL is tangent to the frontier
• Under CAPM assumptions: T = market portfolio
📐 Capital Allocation Line (CAL)
Capital Allocation Line (CAL)
E[rp] = rf + E[rT] − rfσT · σp
Slope = Sharpe Ratio of the Tangency Portfolio
Investor choice: fraction a in T, (1−a) in risk-free
E[rp] = (1−a)·rf + a·E[rT]  |  σp = a·σT
Two-Fund Separation Theorem: Every optimal investor (regardless of risk aversion) holds a combination of the risk-free asset and the Tangency Portfolio. The only difference is the fraction a: more risk-averse → small a, more risk-tolerant → large a (possibly a>1 = leverage).
🔧 Lagrange Optimisation (Exam-relevant)
Minimisation problem: min σ²_p = x'Σx s.t. x'μ = μ̄ (target return) x'1 = 1 (budget constraint) Lagrangian: L = x'Σx − λ₁(x'μ − μ̄) − λ₂(x'1 − 1) Solution via ∂L/∂x = 0 → system of equations defining the efficient frontier
⚠️ EXAM TIP: From the lecture slides: "MAXIMUM OF POINTS → INTERPRET RESULTS!!!" Always explain after the calculation: What does this portfolio mean? Why is the Sharpe Ratio high/low? Which stock dominates and why?
📊 Building the Efficient Frontier – Step by Step

1. Compute E[r] and σ for all individual securities. 2. Compute covariance matrix Σ (or pairwise Cov for 2 stocks). 3. Vary weights x₁ and x₂=(1−x₁) and compute σ²_p for each combination. 4. Plot the σ-E[r] curve → parabola shape. 5. GMVP: point of minimum σ. 6. Efficient portion: from GMVP upward. 7. Tangency: maximum Sharpe Ratio (= slope of CAL through r_f).

❓ Exam Questions – Module 6
What is the Two-Fund Separation Theorem and what does it imply?▼
In a world with a risk-free asset, all optimal investors combine only two "funds": the risk-free asset and the Tangency Portfolio. Regardless of individual risk aversion, the optimal equity allocation is the same (= Tangency Portfolio) – only the fraction varies. Implication: there is no need for customised stock portfolios. An index fund (≈ market portfolio) + risk-free asset is optimal for every investor. Foundation of passive investment strategies.
Explain the difference between the CAL and the Efficient Frontier.▼
Efficient Frontier: the set of all efficient portfolios from risky assets (without risk-free asset). Parabolic curve in σ-E[r]-space. CAL (Capital Allocation Line): straight line through r_f and a risky portfolio. Slope = Sharpe Ratio. The optimal CAL is the one tangent to the frontier (highest Sharpe Ratio) – its tangent point is the Tangency Portfolio T. With a risk-free asset, portfolios on the CAL are achievable and always better (same or higher return) than portfolios on the frontier (except at T).
Given: r_f=4%, E[r_T]=14%, σ_T=20%. Investor targets σ=12%. What is E[r]?▼
On the CAL: a = σ_p/σ_T = 12/20 = 0.60. E[r_p] = r_f + a·(E[r_T]−r_f) = 4% + 0.60·(14%−4%) = 4% + 6% = 10%. Interpretation: 60% in Tangency Portfolio, 40% in risk-free asset. Sharpe Ratio = (14%−4%)/20% = 0.50. Check: SR = (10%−4%)/12% = 0.50 ✓.
Module 7

CAPM, WACC & Cost of Capital

Security Market Line · COC · WACC · APT · Fama-French

📐 CAPM – Capital Asset Pricing Model
CAPM – Capital Asset Pricing Model
ri = rf + βi · (rm − rf)
rf = risk-free rate (government bond)  |  βi = systematic risk  |  (rm−rf) = market risk premium ≈ 6–8% historically
SML (Security Market Line):
Straight line in (β, E[r])-space
• Above SML: undervalued → α > 0 (buy!)
• Below SML: overvalued → α < 0 (avoid!)
• Applies to all assets
CAL vs. SML – Difference:
CAL: x-axis = σ (total risk)
SML: x-axis = β (systematic risk)
CAL: for optimal portfolios
SML: for fair pricing of individual assets
CAPM Example

r_f=3%, ERP=7%, β=1.4 → r_CAPM = 3% + 1.4 × 7% = 3% + 9.8% = 12.8%. A stock with this beta should return 12.8%. Current market return = 15% → stock above SML → undervalued (Alpha = +2.2%).

💼 Cost of Capital (COC – unlevered)
COC – unlevered cost of capital
rassets = DV·rdebt + EV·requity
V = D + E (market values)  |  without tax effect
🏗️ WACC – Weighted Average Cost of Capital
WACC – Weighted Average Cost of Capital
WACC = DV·rd·(1−tc) + EV·re
D/V = debt weight (market values!)  |  E/V = equity weight
rd = cost of debt (pre-tax)  |  tc = tax rate  |  re = cost of equity (via CAPM)
WACC < COC → Tax Shield reduces the effective cost of debt
Important: Calculate WACC using market values (not book values)! D/V and E/V are market-value weights. WACC changes when the capital structure changes.
WACC Example

Market cap: €600m. Debt: €400m. r_d=5%, r_e=12%, t_c=30%. V=€1,000m. D/V=0.4, E/V=0.6. WACC = 0.4 × 5% × 0.70 + 0.6 × 12% = 1.40% + 7.20% = 8.60%.

📊 Estimating r_E – Beta Method
Steps for CAPM-based r_E estimation: 1. Collect historical stock returns and market returns 2. OLS regression: r_i = α + β·r_m + ε → estimates β 3. Determine r_f (current government bond yield) 4. Estimate ERP (historically or implied) 5. r_e = r_f + β̂ · ERP
🔬 APT & Fama-French Faktormodelle
APT (Arbitrage Pricing Theory): E[r_i] = r_f + Σ_k β_{i,k} · λ_k λ_k = risk premium for factor k → Allows multiple risk factors (not just the market)
Fama-French 3-Factor Model: E[r_i] = r_f + β_i · MKT + s_i · SMB + h_i · HML MKT = Market excess return (r_m − r_f) SMB = Small minus Big (size premium: small firms > large) HML = High minus Low (value premium: high B/P > low B/P)

❓ Exam Questions – Module 7
What is the difference between COC and WACC?▼
COC (unlevered) = r_assets = weighted cost of equity and debt without the tax effect. WACC = weighted cost with the tax benefit on debt (interest is tax-deductible → Tax Shield). WACC < COC. Use COC when you value a project as if it were all-equity financed. Use WACC when you want to incorporate the financing effect (debt tax shield) into the discount rate. WACC method: NPV = FCF discounted at WACC (implicitly includes the tax shield).
Stock above the SML vs. below the SML – what does it mean?▼
The SML shows the fair expected return for a given beta. Stock above SML: current expected return > CAPM return → positive alpha → stock is undervalued (offers too much for its risk) → buy until price rises and return falls back to SML. Stock below SML: current expected return < CAPM return → negative alpha → overvalued → sell/avoid. In efficient markets there are no persistent deviations from the SML (arbitrage drives prices back to SML).
How do you calculate WACC in practice for a listed company?▼
1. E (equity market value) = stock price × shares outstanding. 2. D (debt market value) = market value of all debt (often book value ≈ market value). 3. V = E + D, compute E/V and D/V. 4. r_e via CAPM: estimate β from regression, r_e = r_f + β·ERP. 5. r_d = yield on current corporate bonds or cost of debt. 6. t_c = tax rate. 7. WACC = (D/V)·r_d·(1−t_c) + (E/V)·r_e. Typical values: r_e 10–15%, r_d 3–7%, WACC 7–12%.
How do APT and CAPM differ?▼
CAPM: one factor (market portfolio), theoretically grounded (equilibrium model), requires the market portfolio. APT: multiple factors (macroeconomic: GDP, inflation, interest rates…), based on no-arbitrage instead of equilibrium, market portfolio not required. APT is more general, but factors must be identified (not theoretically prescribed). Fama-French is essentially an empirically motivated APT with three specific factors. In practice: CAPM for simple applications, FF3F for empirical research.
